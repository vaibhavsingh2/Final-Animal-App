Steps Taken by Government to Protect Wildlife and Its Habitats


The Government of India has implemented numerous measures to safeguard wildlife and their habitats, preventing any recent extinctions. These efforts encompass initiatives such as protected area creation, wildlife conservation programs, anti-poaching measures, habitat restoration, and public awareness campaigns, all aimed at ensuring the conservation of India's diverse fauna. Following are the steps taken by the government to conserve the fauna:


Wild Life (Protection) Act, 1972: The Wildlife (Protection) Act, 1972 is an important legislation enacted by the Government of India to protect and conserve wildlife and their habitats. It aims to prevent the hunting, poaching, and trade of endangered species, as well as the destruction of their natural habitats. The act provides legal measures for the establishment of protected areas, regulation of hunting, control of wildlife trade, and the appointment of authorities responsible for wildlife conservation. It has played a crucial role in the preservation of India's rich biodiversity and the conservation of threatened species.

Environment Protection Act, 1986: The Environment Protection Act, 1986 is a crucial legislation enacted by the Government of India to protect and improve the quality of the environment. Its primary objective is to safeguard and promote environmental sustainability by preventing and controlling pollution, conserving natural resources, and addressing environmental issues. The act empowers the central government to establish standards and guidelines for various industries, regulate hazardous substances, and take necessary measures to protect and restore environmental quality. It also provides for the establishment of environmental authorities, the enforcement of environmental laws, and the imposition of penalties for non-compliance. The Environment Protection Act, 1986 serves as a foundation for environmental governance and the protection of ecosystems and public health in India.

To learn more about it click here.


Project Elephant: Project Elephant is a centrally sponsored scheme launched by the Government of India in 1992 with the aim of protecting and conserving elephants and their habitats. The project focuses on addressing various issues related to elephant conservation, including habitat management, elephant protection and welfare, mitigating human-elephant conflicts, and creating awareness among local communities. It aims to ensure the long-term survival and well-being of elephants in India by implementing scientific and sustainable measures for their conservation.

To learn more about it click here.


Project Tiger: Project Tiger is a conservation initiative launched in India in 1973 with the objective of protecting and conserving the Bengal tiger, a critically endangered species. The project focuses on the preservation of tiger habitats, prevention of poaching and illegal trade, and scientific monitoring of tiger populations. It aims to ensure the long-term survival of tigers in their natural habitats by establishing tiger reserves, implementing anti-poaching measures, promoting community participation, and raising awareness about tiger conservation. Project Tiger has played a crucial role in the recovery and stabilization of tiger populations in India.

To learn more about it click here.


Biological Diversity Act, 2002: The Biological Diversity Act, 2002 is an important legislation enacted by the Government of India to conserve and promote the sustainable use of biological diversity. The act aims to protect the biodiversity of India, including its ecosystems, species, and genetic resources. It provides a legal framework for the conservation, sustainable utilization, and equitable sharing of benefits arising from the use of biological resources. The act establishes the National Biodiversity Authority (NBA) as the central regulatory body responsible for granting approvals, issuing guidelines, and monitoring activities related to biodiversity conservation and access to genetic resources. It also encourages the establishment of Biodiversity Management Committees (BMCs) at the local level to promote community participation in biodiversity conservation. The Biological Diversity Act, 2002 plays a crucial role in the conservation and sustainable utilization of India's rich biological resources.

To learn more about it click here.


Forest Conservation Act, 1980: The Forest (Conservation) Act, 1980 is a legislation enacted by the Government of India to regulate the diversion of forest land for non-forest purposes. The act aims to protect and conserve forests by requiring prior approval from the central government for any diversion of forest land for activities like mining, industry, infrastructure development, and agriculture. It ensures that the ecological and environmental value of forests is considered before any such diversion takes place. The act provides guidelines for compensatory afforestation, which is the practice of replanting or rehabilitating degraded forest land to offset the loss of forest cover. The Forest (Conservation) Act, 1980 serves as a crucial tool for the sustainable management and conservation of India's forest resources.

To learn more about it click here.


Prevention of Cruelty to Animals Act: The Prevention of Cruelty to Animals Act is a legislation enacted by the Government of India to prevent cruelty towards animals and ensure their welfare. The act provides legal provisions and guidelines for the protection of animals against unnecessary pain, suffering, and exploitation. It covers a wide range of activities, including animal testing, transportation, exhibitions, performances, and slaughtering. The act prohibits cruelty to animals and imposes penalties for offenses committed against them. It also establishes bodies such as the Animal Welfare Board of India to promote animal welfare and oversee the implementation of the act. The Prevention of Cruelty to Animals Act aims to ensure the humane treatment and well-being of animals in India.

To learn more about it click here.


Wildlife Crime Control Bureau: The Wildlife Crime Control Bureau (WCCB) is a statutory multi-disciplinary body established by the Government of India under the Wildlife Protection Act, 1972. It is responsible for combating organized wildlife crime in the country. The WCCB works in close collaboration with various law enforcement agencies, intelligence agencies, and international organizations to prevent and control wildlife-related crimes such as poaching, illegal trade in wildlife and wildlife products, smuggling, and other offenses covered under the Wildlife Protection Act. The bureau conducts investigations, collects intelligence, coordinates efforts with state agencies, and assists in the prosecution of wildlife criminals. The Wildlife Crime Control Bureau plays a crucial role in protecting India's wildlife and biodiversity by addressing wildlife crime effectively.

National Parks: National parks in India are protected areas that are designated and managed by the government for the conservation of India's diverse ecosystems, wildlife, and cultural heritage. These parks serve as vital habitats for a wide range of species and play a significant role in preserving the country's biodiversity.

National parks in India are characterized by their unique landscapes, including forests, grasslands, wetlands, mountains, and coastal areas. They are established to maintain the ecological balance and integrity of these natural environments, protecting native flora and fauna and conserving their habitats.

The primary objective of national parks in India is the preservation of natural resources and the promotion of sustainable practices. These parks have legal safeguards in place to ensure their protection, and activities that may harm the environment or disturb wildlife are restricted.

In addition to their conservation significance, national parks in India also offer opportunities for scientific research, environmental education, and nature-based tourism. Visitors can engage in activities like wildlife spotting, birdwatching, hiking, and camping, while adhering to guidelines that prioritize the well-being of the ecosystems and wildlife.

In total there are 106 existing national parks in India covering an area of 44,378 km square (17,134 sq million), which is 1.35% of the geographical area of the country (National Wildlife Database, Dec. 2020).

Wildlife Sanctuary: Wildlife sanctuaries are protected areas designated by governments to provide a safe haven for wildlife and their habitats. These areas are specifically set aside for the conservation and protection of wildlife species, allowing them to thrive in a relatively undisturbed environment. Wildlife sanctuaries are established to safeguard biodiversity, preserve ecological balance, and provide spaces for scientific research, education, and eco-tourism.

In wildlife sanctuaries, human activities that may harm or disturb wildlife are strictly regulated or prohibited. These areas serve as important refuges for endangered or threatened species, allowing them to breed, feed, and migrate without significant human interference. Wildlife sanctuaries often encompass diverse ecosystems such as forests, grasslands, wetlands, and coastal areas, providing habitats for a wide range of flora and fauna.

In India, wildlife sanctuaries are governed by the Wildlife Protection Act of 1972. The country has numerous wildlife sanctuaries, each with its own unique characteristics and resident species. Some well-known wildlife sanctuaries in India include Ranthambore Wildlife Sanctuary, Periyar Wildlife Sanctuary, Bandipur National Park, and Kanha National Park. These sanctuaries play a vital role in preserving India's rich biodiversity and promoting the conservation of wildlife.
